About Roofing

Roofing History

Roofing has been around for a long time. The earliest roots of roofing can be traced back to Siberia in 40,000 BC1. In 100 BC, Romans introduced a variety of slate and tile-based roofs, usually over open-air homes and amphitheaters. Throughout history, roofing has evolved from straws or twigs to types of metals and tiles tiles. Ancient Chinese civilizations are credited with inventing clay roofing tile sometime around 10,000 B.C. The invention of clay tile roofs was key in many ways. The roofs were flame-resistant, water-resistant, and durable enough to last more than 100 years.

Wood and slate shingles and clay tiles were the predominant roofing choice until the mid-19th century when metal and bituminous roofing systems made low-slope applications possible. During the 20th century, several new materials were developed for low- and steep-sloped roofs. Today, there are many different types of roofing materials available including asphalt shingles, metal roofing, wood shakes and shingles, clay and concrete tiles, slate, rubber slate, and more. Renovate Robotics focuses on installing asphalt shingles.

Shingling Steps

  1. Measure and mark the roof area where you want to install the shingles. You will need to cut some shingles to fit the edges and corners of the roof.
  2. Install the starter strip along the lower edge of the roof, overlapping the eaves by about 1/4 inch. This will prevent water from seeping under the shingles and damaging the roof deck.
  3. Nail the first row of shingles on top of the starter strip, using four nails per shingle. The nails should be about 3/4 inch from each edge and 1 inch above the cutouts. Make sure the shingles are aligned with the edge of the roof and each other.
  4. Install the second row of shingles, offsetting them by half a shingle width from the first row. This will create a staggered pattern that improves the appearance and performance of the roof. Continue nailing the shingles in rows, working your way up to the ridge of the roof.
  5. Cut and install ridge cap shingles along the peak of the roof, overlapping them by at least 4 inches. Nail them down with two nails on each side, about 1 inch from the edge.
  6. Inspect your work and make sure there are no gaps, loose nails, or damaged shingles. If you find any problems, fix them as soon as possible.

Roofing Safety

Roofing is one of the most dangerous occupations in the construction industry. Roofers are exposed to various hazards, such as falls, electrocution, heat stroke, and injuries from tools and equipment. According to the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA), roofing workers have a fatality rate of 51.5 per 100,000 full-time workers, which is more than 10 times the national average and ranks among the top ten most dangerous jobs in America. Therefore, it is essential for roofers to follow safety requirements and best practices to prevent accidents and injuries. Some of the safety requirements for roofers include:

  • Wearing appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE), such as hard hats, gloves, boots, eye protection, and harnesses.
  • Using fall protection systems, such as guardrails, safety nets, or personal fall arrest systems, when working at heights of six feet or more.
  • Inspecting and maintaining tools and equipment regularly and using them properly.
  • Avoiding contact with power lines and other electrical sources.
  • Staying hydrated and taking breaks in shaded areas to prevent heat-related illnesses.
  • Reporting any unsafe conditions or incidents to supervisors or managers.

By following these safety requirements, roofers can reduce the risks of serious or fatal injuries and ensure a safe and productive work environment.
